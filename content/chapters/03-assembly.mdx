Precision work on Earth. Panels nest flat for launch. Orbital assembly
guides each face into place with magnetic alignment, then aluminum
welding seals the joints so the shell becomes a single pressure
boundary — not a module stack glued together.

### The sequence

**Fabricate.** Every one of the 32 panels is built on Earth to the same
specification. Frames milled from **6061-T6 aluminium**, lockbolt hole
pattern to **NAS9306C-06**, gaskets laid into dual fluorosilicone
channels. No one-off joints.

**Stack.** Panels nest flat for the launch fairing. Face centers align
to the canonical shell on first deployment — nothing is re-measured in
orbit.

**Launch.** Mass budget respects the canonical **2.25 m** edge and
**140 mm** total frame depth. Panels ride stowed, not pre-assembled.

**Snap.** Magnetic alignment rings around each face's edges pull
neighbouring panels into their canonical relative angle. Any two faces
meet at the dihedral angle the truncated-icosahedron math fixes for
them — there is no field-adjustable joint.

**Weld.** Aluminium-to-aluminium friction-stir or laser welds seal the
joints once aligned. The gasket stack handles operational pressure; the
weld handles lifetime structural integrity.

### Why repeatability matters

The same mechanical language repeats 32 times — so every crew that
follows can swap panels without re-learning the habitat. Precedent
shows the opposite: ISS modules each have their own proprietary seal,
fastener, and service interface. HCSA uses **one** joint, everywhere.

| Task | ISS precedent | HCSA |
|---|---|---|
| Panel swap | Module-specific tooling | One joint, one tool |
| Seal spares | Per-module stock | One gasket SKU |
| Crew re-train | Yes, per module | No, one panel |
| Joint validation | N per module | One + instances |
