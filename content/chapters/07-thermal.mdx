The axial core is the vein system of the habitat. Red loops carry warm
water away from thermal collectors; blue returns chilled distillate.
Passive solar distillation targets **~15 L/day**, and a closed loop
cycles **480–720 L/day** back through hygiene, irrigation, and
drinking.

Split the beam and the plumbing is not hidden — it is the point.

### The water loop

| Flow | Rate | Role |
|---|---|---|
| Passive distillation output | **~15 L/day** | Fresh water top-up |
| Closed-loop recirculation | **480–720 L/day** | Hygiene + irrigation + drinking |
| Loop mode | Red → heat reject · Blue → chilled return | Thermal + potable |
| Greenhouse demand | Continuous | Transpiration returns to loop |

The red loops glow warmer at peak thermal load; the blue loops pulse
brighter as distillate comes off the condenser. The **1.25 Hz
alternating pulse** in this phase is real: red and blue run 180° out of
phase so crew can read active-vs-idle at a glance.

### Why split it, why show it

A single trunk beam is the simplest plumbing route for a habitat this
size. The beam **scales 3×** during this phase not because the pipe
grows — it's a visualisation choice that says "this is the thing the
rest of the habitat attaches to". Everything else (greenhouses,
galley, observation, sleep) is a consumer of what this core moves.

### What closure actually requires

- **Thermal.** Radiator area scales with hot-side temperature to the
  fourth power. A warmer loop needs smaller radiators, but every
  degree hotter also raises CO₂ scrubber work. Trade is ongoing.
- **Water.** Above ~95 % closure, a single seal failure dominates the
  mass budget. Redundant distillation and a reserve bladder are in the
  design, not sized here.
- **Power.** The 20 transparent-PV faces provide baseline generation;
  the 20 sliding-opaque-solar shades on the same hexes give boost at
  the cost of local opacity.

Staged prototyping is how these tradeoffs become real — which is the
next phase.
